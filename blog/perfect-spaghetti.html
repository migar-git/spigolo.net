Technique

How to Cook Perfect Spaghetti

Published February 5, 2026

Cooking pasta seems simple — boil water, add pasta, drain. But the difference between good pasta and great pasta comes down to technique. Here's how Italian chefs achieve that perfect al dente texture every time.

1. Use Plenty of Water

The golden rule: 4-6 quarts of water per pound of pasta. Pasta releases starch as it cooks, and too little water means that starch concentrates in the pot, making pasta gummy.

More water = more dilution = pasta that stays separate and cooks evenly.

2. Salt Generously

Your pasta water should taste like the sea. This isn't an exaggeration — aim for about 1-2 tablespoons of salt per gallon of water.

Why so much? Pasta absorbs water as it cooks, and that water flavors the pasta from within. Undersalted water = undersalted pasta, and you can't fix it after the fact.

When to add salt: After the water comes to a boil, right before adding pasta. Adding it earlier doesn't save time and can damage your pot.

3. Don't Add Oil

Contrary to popular belief, adding oil to pasta water does nothing useful. It floats on the surface and doesn't prevent sticking. Worse, it can prevent sauce from adhering later.

Want to prevent sticking? Use enough water and stir occasionally during the first 2 minutes of cooking.

4. Understand Al Dente

"Al dente" literally means "to the tooth" — pasta that's cooked through but still has a slight resistance when you bite it.

The test: Start tasting 2-3 minutes before the package time. You want pasta that's no longer hard or chalky in the center, but still has a bit of firmness.

For artisan pasta, this usually means:

  • Thin shapes (spaghetti, linguine): 7-9 minutes
  • Medium shapes (penne, rigatoni): 10-12 minutes
  • Thick shapes (paccheri): 12-14 minutes

But always taste — different brands and shapes vary significantly.

5. Reserve Pasta Water

Before draining, scoop out at least 1 cup of pasta cooking water. This starchy liquid is liquid gold for finishing sauces.

The starch in the water acts as an emulsifier, helping oil-based and tomato-based sauces cling to pasta. It's the secret to restaurant-quality pasta at home.

6. Never Rinse (Usually)

Rinsing pasta washes away the surface starch that helps sauce adhere. The only exception: if you're making a cold pasta salad.

7. Finish in the Sauce

Here's the technique that separates home cooks from pros: finish cooking the pasta in the sauce.

The method:

  1. Cook pasta until 1-2 minutes shy of al dente
  2. Drain (reserve that cooking water!)
  3. Add pasta directly to the pan with your sauce
  4. Add a splash of pasta water
  5. Toss everything together over medium heat for 1-2 minutes

What this does: The pasta absorbs the sauce while finishing cooking, and the starch helps bind everything together. The result is pasta and sauce that are truly unified, not just mixed.

8. Don't Overload the Sauce

In Italy, pasta is lightly sauced — you should see the pasta, not drown it. A good ratio is about 1½ to 2 cups of sauce per pound of pasta.

Oversaucing is a very American habit. Let the pasta be the star.

9. Serve Immediately

Pasta waits for no one. Have your table set, your guests seated, and your cheese grated before you start cooking.

Pasta begins to absorb sauce and lose its ideal texture within minutes. In Italian households, whoever is cooking has absolute authority to demand everyone sit down NOW.

The Perfect Recipe: Spaghetti Aglio e Olio

This classic Roman dish uses every technique above and showcases great pasta beautifully.

Ingredients (serves 2):

  • 200g bronze-die spaghetti
  • 6 cloves garlic, thinly sliced
  • 6 tablespoons extra-virgin olive oil
  • 1 teaspoon red pepper flakes
  • ¼ cup chopped fresh parsley
  • Salt to taste

Method:

  1. Bring 4 quarts of water to a boil. Add 2 tablespoons salt.
  2. While water heats, gently cook garlic and pepper flakes in olive oil over low heat until garlic is golden (not brown) — about 3-4 minutes.
  3. Cook spaghetti until 1 minute shy of al dente. Reserve 1 cup pasta water.
  4. Add pasta directly to the garlic oil pan with ½ cup pasta water.
  5. Toss over medium-high heat for 1-2 minutes, adding more water if needed, until sauce emulsifies and coats pasta.
  6. Remove from heat, add parsley, toss, and serve immediately.

No cheese. No modifications. Just six ingredients and perfect technique.

With bronze-die pasta, you'll taste every nuance: the wheaty flavor, the garlic, the olive oil, the way the rough texture holds onto every drop. This is what great pasta is all about.
